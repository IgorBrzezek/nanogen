History and Evolution of Cellular Networks

From 1G to 4G – Foundations of Modern Connectivity

Before we discuss complex 5G systems, we must understand how mobile technology has evolved over the past 40 years. Each "generation" (G) brought a breakthrough in both technical and user experience.

  • 1G (Analog): Voice only, no standards (NMT, AMPS).
  • 2G (Digital): GSM – the first worldwide standard, SMS, encryption.
  • 3G (Multimedia): UMTS – internet access, video calls.
  • 4G (IP-Native): LTE – a network based entirely on data packets.

In this presentation, we will focus on how the network is built "from the inside out," starting with the legendary GSM standard.

2/5

1G – The Analog Era (1980s)

First Steps of Mobility

First-generation networks were based on analog FM (Frequency Modulation) transmission. The most well-known system in Poland was NMT 450 (Nordic Mobile Telephone), operated by the Centertel network.

  • Frequency: 450 MHz (large range, low capacity).
  • Flaws: No encryption (conversations could be eavesdropped with a regular radio), no compatibility between countries.
  • Terminal: Suitcase-sized "phones," installed mainly in cars.

However, 1G proved that people want to talk while on the move, which forced the creation of a digital standard.

3/5

2G – The GSM Revolution (Global System for Mobile)

The World's Digital Standard

The GSM standard (originally "Groupe Spécial Mobile") was created as a European initiative to unify cellular systems. It became the most popular standard in history.

  • Digital transmission: Sound converted to bits, allowing compression and better quality.
  • Security: Introduction of the SIM card and radio signal encryption (A5 algorithm).
  • Roaming: Ability to use the same phone in different countries and with different operators.
  • New feature: Short Message Service (SMS).
4/5

GSM – System Architecture (Division)

Three Main Subsystems

The GSM network is not a monolith. It consists of three cooperating areas:

  • BSS (Base Station Subsystem): The radio part we see every day (towers).
  • NSS (Network and Switching Subsystem): The network core, the heart of operations handling call switching.
  • OSS (Operation Support Subsystem): Management and monitoring systems for network operation by technicians.

Understanding these layers is crucial for an engineer, as each one is responsible for a different stage of the connection.

5/5

BSS – Base Station Subsystem

The Link Between Phone and Network

The BSS subsystem consists of two key elements:

  • BTS (Base Transceiver Station): The base station. A physical tower with antennas and radios that communicates directly with the phone (Um interface).
  • BSC (Base Station Controller): The base station controller. One BSC manages dozens of BTS stations. It is responsible for allocating radio channels and deciding on handovers (switching a user between towers).

The BTS is "dumb" – it only performs radio tasks. The intelligence (control logic) resides in the BSC.
